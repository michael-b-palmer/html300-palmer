Project Brief

DocuSign provides a variety of tools for managing accounts and users. While all administrators have access to basic settings, DocuSign Admin represents a complex, enterprise-level set of admin tools.

As DocuSign continues to grow, the number of users introduced to DocuSign Admin is increasing. I was tasked with finding a way to help smooth this transition.

The Problem

Because DocuSign Admin is separate from the basic tools with which most admins are familiar, adding this new set of tools can be quite overwhelming. With a steady stream of new users encountering DocuSign Admin for the first time, we need to reduce the time required between account creation and productivity.

Additionally, 20% of enterprise customers aren't using features they've paid for. This causes customer confusion and leads to unnecessary support cases and churn.

Goals

  • Understand the existing user experience
  • Promote awareness and adoption of existing features
  • Increase self-service opportunities and reduce support case volume
  • Reduce time between organization creation and milestones

SME Interviews

During the discovery phase of the project, I worked alongside a product manager as we interviewed several Customer Success Architects (CSAs). These are the folks out there in the trenches with our customers every day, delighting in their successes and sharing in their pain. We asked them to share some of that pain with us, and we got an earful from each participant.

We spoke with seven CSAs in total, and quickly noticed a few common themes:

  • The concept of two different sets of admin tools (basic and complex) is not well understood
  • New users are often overwhelmed and have no idea what to do first
  • In their confusion, new users often make 'mistakes' that are painful to correct
  • Many existing users miss out on features due to a lack of awareness

User Flow Mapping

Ensuring a customer sets up their admin experience correctly is contingent on their understanding of where they are, what they need to do right now, and what they can do in the future.

I mapped out the steps for setting up an organization, drawing on my product knowledge and filling in the gaps with details from the SME interviews.

The map helped us answer countless 'does it do this when I do that?' questions and helped put us all on the same page.

As a result, we were able to focus on the processes and identify pain points and opportunities for improvement.

Workshop

With the map complete, I created a miro board and hosted a workshop with SMEs and the product manager to categorize and quantify the pain points.

Workshop Phases
  1. Capture top concerns
  2. Group concerns by topic
  3. Brainstorm solutions to topic groups and identify benefits
  4. Identify next steps and create an implementation plan

Having clearly identified the problem space, we began to explore potential solutions. We eventually landed on a redesign of the Admin homepage as the best choice for our MVP.

By utilizing this real estate properly, we can establish a journey that customers can look forward to taking with us.

Why the Homepage?
  • It serves as the starting point for new and existing admins
  • The existing version is spartan and lacks guidance or support content
  • It's a cost-effective, light-touch solution with a measurable impact
  • By providing information and guidance to users, we can greatly reduce or eliminate many of the pain points surfaced by the research

Desk Research

Having identified the solution we wanted to explore (a homepage refresh), I wanted to see how other large companies arrange their admin homepages. I reviewed several publicly available enterprise administration experiences.

The first thing that became clear was the need for some sort of guidance or support material on the page. In an overwhelmingly technical space like this, guideposts and directions are essential.

That said, even with guidance, there is just too much content to cover on one page, so progressive disclosure and support content are a must as well.

Finally, allowing admins to customize their dashboard seems to be of great benefit; why tell them what to display when you can ask them what they want to see instead?

Defining Success Metrics

We measured several baseline metrics and will continue to monitor for results. We also implemented Mixpanel tracking within the page to collect additional data.

  • Onboarding: Time elapsed between account creation and milestone tasks
  • Adoption: Percentage of customers who use most or all of their paid features
  • Usability: Customer interactions on the home page and linked pages (clicks, bounce, etc.)
  • Discoverability: Percentage of customers viewing the Features page
  • Self-service: Views to linked support content as well as the number of related support cases

Wireframing

Great - so now we know that we need to share something informational on the homepage, what should we focus on?

This is where we combined the insights from the discovery phase with Mixpanel metrics from the existing homepage. What are users currently clicking on? What areas are they either ignoring or missing?

With this knowledge in hand, I sketched out several wireframes to explore different layouts and components.

Areas of Exploration
  • A checklist or wizard to guide the setup process
  • A 'completeness' tracker to gamify the experience
  • A banner image with a personalized greeting
  • Timely notifications to surface items needing attention
  • Dynamic content to display the current status
  • Static resources to provide context and support
